About

Welcome! I am Aaron, a 4th year Electrical Engineering student at the University of Michigan with 5 years of industry experience with Additive Manufacturing .

My interest with electromechanical devices began when I began modifying my foam dart guns to have more capabilities, such as better accuracy, higher fire rates, and ammo counting capabilities. After getting much positive feedback from that project, I began learning more about controls and embedded systems, laying the foundation for my work in neurally enabled prosthetic devices. I began working on my prosthetic arm designs in high school, first developing my own amplifiers and control circuits and code, and eventually designing and fabricating a full arm mainly created with additive technologies.

My interest in additive technologies has taken me through 5 internships where I have had the opportunity to work on everything from powder development for laser systems, to end use parts to be implemented. In addition to this, I have presented my own work at the RAPID industry conference for 3 consecutive years.

In my free time, I enjoy exploring on my Electric Longboard, learning about clocks and timekeeping, collaborating with people on creative projects, and eating yummy food.



Additive Manufacturing Experience

Render of print in place fully functioning wind up clock

Render of 3D printed cybernetic arm with neural interface

I have completed 3 internships with a direct focus in additive, and additionally have done numerous projects incorporating additive to a variety of problem sets. I have experience with nearly all types of additive technologies, ranging from mold production with Stratasys digital ABS, to metal component production with the 3DSystems ProX DMP 320 and the EOS M100. In addition to this I also have experience developing new metal powders for AM applications using the Tekna 15kW ICP powder spheroidization system. My metal powders experience extends also to the characterization of both raw powders and sintered parts. This includes equipment such as Microtrac Particle characterization, LECO gas chromatography equipment, mass spectrometry, and rheological analysis.

In addition to formal internships, I have also been to India twice to set up prototyping and short run manufacturing capabilities for Aurolab, the manufacturing division of the Aravind Eye Hospitals. My responsibilities were to lay out the scope of the project, budget, safety procedures, and training. In order to accomplish this, plans were digitally created and shared while I was in the US, and I also traveled on-site to train personnel and ensure all proper procedures were in place for manufacturing to occur.

3D Printed Pediatric Prosthetic Device


My goal with this was to design, build, and share how to build low cost, neurally interfacing, prosthetic devices. Work began on my own in high school where I designed my first signal amplifiers and built my first arm. Work continued when I came to University and started my own club within which I lead a team of ~50 students to design, build, and deploy these devices to people in need.

This arm was designed, manufactured, and delivered by my club at the University of Michigan for $100. It is controlled by the user's peripheral nervous system and is tough enough for everyday use.

SmartBins

SmartBins was a hackathon project where we set out to solve the problem of not knowing the overhead bin capacity on a plane during boarding. Our solution was a hardware array that would be in the plane's overhead bins connecting via an arduino to a webpage built in d3 to show the real time bin capacity and total plane capacity.

We won Top 8 of over 1000 other students as well as Delta's first prize of Best Air Travel Solution

Lead to Pixels

I developed this virtual reality world for my class Technology in Sustainable Communities where we chose the final project and determined the success criteria. I chose to make a VR world of the proposed Cook Park so the residents of that area and anyone with access to a web browser would be able to look at what the park would look like and virutally walk around in it.

Use W S A D to walk around and click the mouse and drag to look around the park on desktop. Tap to walk and rotate your mobile device to look around. It is also compatable with phone VR headsets.

Print in Place Clock

This print in place clock was designed and built to start pushing the boundaries of what can be done with additive technologies. The idea is to have a part that people will immediately understand something interesting is happening when handling it. This demonstrates a simplification of the supply chain, consolidation of parts, different material properties based on geometry (spring vs frame vs gears) and reduction of assembly steps needed for an end use component.

Aravind Eye Hospitals Additve Manufacturing

In January of 2017 I was asked by my research colleagues if I wanted to go to India to build additive capabilities for a medical manufacturing group with strong ties to the University of Michigan. I then spent the next 10 weeks planning until I found myself on a plane to Madurai, Tamil Nadu, in May. When I arrived, my responsibilities were to set up equipment, iron out workflows, train personnel, and solidify multiple supply chain sources to ensure the machines were kept running.

I returned to India in 2018 to evaluate the work that had been done for the past year and to add additional capabilities to the facilities. The personnel that I had trained were still learning new things and applying them to interesting problems that arose around the manufacturing center.
