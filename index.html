About

Welcome! I am Aaron, a 4th year Electrical Engineering student at the University of Michigan with 5 years of industry experience in Additive Manufacturing .

I became interested in electromechanical devices when I began modifying my foam dart guns to have better accuracy, higher fire rates, and ammo counting capabilities. After getting much positive feedback from that project, I learned more about controls and embedded systems, laying the foundation for my work in neural controlled prosthetic devices. I started building my prosthetic arm designs in high school, first developing my own amplifiers and control circuits and code. Not long after, I designed and fabricated a full arm, using additive technologies.

My interest in additive technologies has taken me through five internships, which gave me the opportunity to work on everything from powder development (for laser systems) to end-use parts for production. In addition to this, I have presented my own work at the RAPID industry conference for three consecutive years.

In my free time, I enjoy exploring on my electric longboard, learning about clocks and timekeeping, collaborating with people on creative projects, and eating yummy food.

3D Printed Pediatric Prosthetic Device


My goal with this was to design, build, and share how to build low cost, neurally interfacing, prosthetic devices. Work began on my own in high school where I designed my first signal amplifiers and built my first arm. Work continued when I came to University and started my own club within which I lead a team of ~50 students to design, build, and deploy these devices to people in need.

This arm was designed, manufactured, and delivered by my club at the University of Michigan for $100. It is controlled by the user's peripheral nervous system and is tough enough for everyday use.

SmartBins

SmartBins was a hackathon project where we set out to solve the problem of not knowing the overhead bin capacity on a plane during boarding. Our solution was a hardware array that would be in the plane's overhead bins connecting via an arduino to a webpage built in d3 to show the real time bin capacity and total plane capacity.

We won Top 8 of over 1000 other students as well as Delta's first prize of Best Air Travel Solution

Lead to Pixels

This project was an exploration into how we can use additive technologies to help conserve historic artifacts, in this case letterpress type. Whether letterpress type is damaged, fragile, or otherwise inaccessible, it inhibits the ability of conservationists and researchers to work with historic type. I worked together with Dr. Rebecca Chung, to build a workflow in which type could be digitally created, printed with metal filled materials, and post processed to a shiny printable finish.

The results of our work is a workflow and set of materials that enables low cost reproduction of historic type, and production of new type in a more widely accessible manner than ever before.

Print in Place Clock

This print in place clock was designed and built to start pushing the boundaries of what can be done with additive technologies. The idea is to have a part that people will immediately understand something interesting is happening when handling it. This demonstrates a simplification of the supply chain, consolidation of parts, different material properties based on geometry (spring vs frame vs gears) and reduction of assembly steps needed for an end use component.

Aravind Eye Hospitals Additve Manufacturing

In January of 2017 I was asked by my research colleagues if I wanted to go to India to build additive capabilities for a medical manufacturing group with strong ties to the University of Michigan. I then spent the next 10 weeks planning until I found myself on a plane to Madurai, Tamil Nadu, in May. Video Game to 3D Print

This project was my first serious foray into desinging an in depth custom workflow for additive. I wanted to start with models of characters from one of my favorite video games, and end with a model that I could have on my desk. This project took me through extracting game files, learning about 3D animation programs, mesh subdivision, mesh sculpting, and file repair to prepare for printing. After printing, I developed a post processing procedure to knock down supports from the printing process, and clean up the model to look more uniform.

I began this by extracting the game files and importing them into Autodesk 3Ds Max. From there I was able to pose my characters, either manually or with animations that I also pulled from in game files. With this posed model, I exported as an obj and repaired the file within Autodesk Netfabb. The repaired files were then brought into Meshlab, where butterfly subdivisions were used to add detail to the models, as video game assets are quite often very low poly, relying on textures to enhance the model. After a rough pass with Meshlab, files were hand sculpted in Autodesk Meshmixer to achieve final part details. After another pass through Netfabb repairs, the part was sent to the Formlabs Form 2 for printing. After printing, parts were removed, cleaned, and cured. Then supports were removed, artifacts on the part were sanded, and the entire part sand blasted for a more even surface finish.
